Cases were identified from civil-liberties reporting, court and zoning records, and news coverage, then catalogued with a controlled vocabulary describing the project, the opposition it met, and how it was resolved.

The pattern

A house of worship is rarely refused for being one.

It is refused for traffic. For parking. For the size of a dome, the height of a minaret, the loss of a tax base, the character of a neighborhood, the safety of an aquifer.

Across four decades, the same objections recur from Idaho to Massachusetts — sometimes sincere, sometimes a lawful-sounding cover for something else. In case after case, the record shows a permit granted to a church quietly denied to a mosque next door.

The cases below are not the loudest. They are the ones that show the range: what communities proposed, what met them, and where the ground finally settled.

A cemetery, a moratorium, and a militia

Newton County, Georgia

A Muslim community's plan for a cemetery and future mosque on rural land drew a county moratorium on new houses of worship and national coverage. One commission meeting was called off over security concerns about an armed demonstration outside. The county later required hearings for large religious sites — but the cemetery opened in 2017.

Opposition: public campaign · zoning moratorium · intimidation

77.40°W EST. 2008
Mosque & community center Litigated & settled

The county that had never said no to a church

Islamic Center of Henrico, Virginia

Henrico County denied a rezoning for a 10,000-square-foot mosque on long-vacant commercial land, citing noise and traffic. The center sued; the U.S. Department of Justice investigated and filed its own federal suit, noting the county had never once denied a zoning application from a Christian church. The case settled in 2011 and the rezoning was approved.

Opposition: administrative denial · federal RLUIPA litigation

Mosque · under construction Destroyed

Burned before it opened

Lake Travis, Texas

A mosque set to open in the spring never did. The site was vandalized, and months later the partially built structure burned to the ground under suspicious circumstances — there were no gas or electric hookups on the property. This is the far end of the spectrum the atlas records: where objection crosses into arson.

Opposition: vandalism · suspected arson
04 40.58°N
74.10°W EST. 2010
Mosque & community center Relocated

Turned away from a convent, welcomed by a temple

Staten Island, New York

A plan to buy a Catholic convent and convert it into a mosque met rallies and fears ranging from traffic to terrorism; the parish board rejected the sale. The community regrouped, bought a former Hindu temple nearby instead, and renovated it. The center opened — a few miles from where it was first refused.

Opposition: public campaign · rallies · sale blocked
05 42.24°N
83.75°W EST. 2011
Islamic school Litigated & settled

Five years to build a schoolhouse

Pittsfield Township, Michigan

The township denied the Michigan Islamic Academy's rezoning for a school on vacant land, citing traffic, after heavily attended hearings. CAIR sued in 2012; the Department of Justice filed a separate RLUIPA suit in 2015. Both settled in 2016 for roughly $1.7 million, clearing the way for the project.

Opposition: administrative denial · public campaign · federal litigation

The battleground Zoning

Most cases are decided not at a ballot box but at a planning commission or board of adjustment — where "traffic" and "parking" can carry a great deal of weight.
